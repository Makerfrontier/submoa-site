Every serious outdoorsman eventually learns the hard way that cheap boots are expensive. You spend less upfront and more later — in replaced pairs, blister care, and the quiet frustration of wet feet when it matters most. When Jim Green approached me about putting their African Ranger line through its paces, I told them I'd give them real use. Months of it. Mud, brush, long walks, short sprints, and everything in between.

The African Ranger with leather midsole in the Bronco Biscuit colorway arrived with the kind of no-nonsense packaging that told me something right away — these aren't trying to impress anyone. They're trying to work.

First Fit

Right out of the box, the leather midsole construction is immediately noticeable. This isn't a sneaker with an EVA footbed and marketing language. The leather midsole breaks in over the first couple of wears, conforming to your foot shape in a way that synthetic materials simply can't replicate. By the third day of walking, the fit was personalized. That matters when you're covering rough ground for hours.

Sizing ran true-to-foot. I wear an 11 in most hunting and outdoor boots, and the 11 in the African Ranger fit exactly as expected — not tight, not sloppy. The toe box has enough room that your toes don't hit the front on a downhill stretch, which is where most field boots fail you.

The Bronco Biscuit colorway is a muted tan-brown that handles dirt and brush scuffs without looking destroyed after a season. Function over aesthetics, but not ugly.

In The Field

I wore these on three separate hunting trips over eight weeks — central Georgia timber, Arkansas river bottom, and high-cut sage country in Wyoming. Different terrain, different conditions, same boots.

Georgia heat and humidity is the real test. Synthetic boots turn your feet into a sauna by noon. The African Ranger's leather construction breathes better than any mesh-and-synthetic I've run. Not cool in the way a performance running shoe is cool, but not suffocating either. By week three, the leather had formed to my foot well enough that I forgot I was wearing them.

The leather midsole also provides a level of stability that many modern boots sacrifice. When you're walking through uneven ground — creek beds, root networks, cutover timber — you feel connected. The boot doesn't flex in ways that make you work harder to stay balanced.

Sole grip was solid on everything from wet grass to loose gravel. The outsole pattern isn't aggressive in the way that hunting-specific lugs can be, but it held reliably on everything I put it on. Mud shed reasonably well — not self-cleaning like some newer rubber compounds, but no significant buildup that affected traction.

Durability

The leather upper showed expected wear after repeated use — some brush marks on the toe and lateral side, a light scuff on the heel counter from repeated setting down on rough surfaces. Nothing that affects function. The stitching on the eyelet row stayed tight throughout testing. No loose threads, no pulled eyelets, no separation at the toe.

The leather midsole has held up without significant compression — a common failure point in boots that see hard use over time. The insole remained supportive, not packed out. I didn't baby these boots and they didn't fall apart.

What Could Be Better

The leather requires maintenance. That's not a knock — it's just the reality of leather boots. If you're the type who thinks saddle soap is optional, these will dry out and crack faster than they should. I conditioned mine twice during the testing period with a standard leather conditioner and the leather stayed supple.

The outsole could be more aggressive for wet-rock crossings. If you're in terrain with a lot of creek crossings or slickrock, the current sole pattern is adequate but not exceptional. A vibram-style lug sole upgrade option would be welcome.

Break-in period is real — plan on a week of regular wear before you take them into the field for a full day. That's normal for quality leather boots but worth knowing if you're used to synthetic boots that work day one.

The Takeaway

Jim Green's African Ranger with leather midsole is a serious boot for serious work. The leather construction, comfortable break-in, and solid durability make it a legitimate option for anyone who spends real time outdoors — hunting, ranch work, guiding, or just regular use on rough terrain.

The price point under $200 puts it in an interesting category — above the casual outdoor boot market but below the premium heritage boot brands. For what you get in field performance, the value is there.

These aren't the lightest boots you'll find, they require maintenance, and they need a break-in period. All of that is by design. They're built for people who use them, not people who display them.

If you're looking for a boot that works and doesn't require babying, put these on your list.
