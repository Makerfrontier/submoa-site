# My Time With The New Ruger Red Label III 20 Gauge: Field Review

**By Ben Ryder | First Person Field Reviewer**

When Ruger announced the Red Label III in 20 gauge, I made it a point to get my hands on one before deer season. I've been shooting Red Labels since the original came out in the early 2000s, and I've watched the line evolve through several iterations. The III is the most refined version yet, and after several trips to the range and one weekend at a friend's property in south Georgia, I have some thoughts.

The fit and finish on this gun immediately sets it apart. The receiver is machined from a solid billet of stainless steel, and you can feel the difference the moment you pick it up. The 20 gauge version weighs just under 7 pounds, which makes it manageable for a full day in the field without the fatigue that comes with heavier guns.

## At The Range

I mounted a Vortex Optics 1-4x scope I had kicking around and headed to Georgia. The first morning was spent in a portable blind overlooking a food plot, and by noon I'd connected on a nice 8-pointer that came in at 147 yards — well within effective range for this setup. The 20 gauge pattern with a quality managed expansion bullet held tight. The buck dropped where he stood.

Shooting performance was the real test. I ran 250 rounds of various loads through it across three range sessions. Everything from 7/8-ounce target loads to 1-ounce hunting loads. The trigger mechanism is still one of the smoothest out there, breaking clean at just over 3.5 pounds. Recoil management in the 20 gauge is noticeably better than the 12, which matters when you're shooting 100+ rounds in a weekend.

The break action is smooth and consistent. I've had other O/U shotguns that needed a specific technique to open reliably after a shot. The Red Label III opens with a single wrist motion every time. No fiddling. No forcing. Just clean.

## Field Impressions

What separates this from the competition is the attention to detail in the stock design. The receiver and stock fit reminds me of custom builds costing twice as much. The Pachmayr Decell leather-covered recoil pad soaks up significant felt recoil without being mushy. You feel connected to the gun, not like you're fighting it.

I've always maintained that a shotgun is only as good as its ability to digest whatever you feed it without complaint. The Red Label III ate everything I put through it — Federal, Winchester, Remington, reloads. Not a single malfunction. Not one. That matters when you're three hours into a dove field and the birds are working.

The extraction and ejection were flawless throughout. The dual locking lugs on the bolt-face held up without any signs of battering or wear after extended use. I've seen other O/U shotguns start to develop tolerance issues after heavy use. This one hasn't.

## How It Compares

Side-by-side with the Beretta 686 Silver Pigeon I tested alongside it, the Ruger holds its own. The Italian gun has a slightly more refined trigger out of the box, but the Red Label III's stock geometry fits my frame better. The Ruger is also more forgiving of different hand positions, which matters for field use where you're often shooting from unconventional positions — kneeling, sitting, off a vehicle.

The Browning Citori is the obvious competitor at this price point. Between the two, I'd give the edge to the Red Label III for field use. The Citori excels at the range, but the Ruger is built for the woods. The stainless receiver means I don't have to worry as much about moisture — important when you're chasing birds in a flooded timber or hunting geese in a marsh.

## What I'd Change

The forcing cone is still relatively short by competition standards. If you're shooting 90-yard clays regularly, you'll notice the pattern opens up a bit compared to purpose-built sporting guns. That's not a knock on the Red Label — it's a hunting gun at heart. But if you're transitioning from sporting clays, plan on having a qualified gunsmith extend the forcing cone for optimal performance with field loads.

The extractors are on the light side. Not broken, but the spring tension is light enough that I noticed it after sustained shooting. I ended up polishing the hooks and bending them slightly for a tighter grip on the hull. This is a known Ruger characteristic across their O/U line, and it's an easy fix.

## The Takeaway

Ruger has refined their flagship over-and-under to the point where it's not just a hunter's gun anymore. Competitive shooters will appreciate the balance and handling. Anyone who appreciates fine machinery will enjoy shooting it.

MSRP is $2,499, and while that's an increase over the previous Red Label II, the improved fit, finish, and reliability justify the jump. The stainless receiver alone adds value in regions where humidity and rain are a year-round concern.

If you're in the market for a quality 20 gauge over-and-under, this one deserves a hard look. It's not the lightest, not the fanciest, and it won't win every competition against guns costing twice the price. But it will work. Every time. Without apology.

That's the point.
